\chapter*{Conclusion}
This project presented the design and implementation of the Quantum Retrieval-Augmented Generation (QRAG) framework, a hybrid quantum-classical system aimed at improving the performance of Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) models in handling syntactic ambiguity. The study identified that while classical RAG systems are effective for linear and keyword-based queries, they struggle with structurally complex and ambiguous inputs due to limitations in traditional parsing mechanisms.

To address this limitation, the proposed QRAG framework integrates Quantum Natural Language Processing (QNLP) techniques with classical retrieval pipelines. The system introduces an Ambiguity Controller that dynamically routes queries based on structural complexity, ensuring that quantum resources are utilized only when necessary. This selective hybrid approach enables the system to balance computational efficiency with improved semantic understanding.

The experimental results demonstrate that the quantum-enhanced pipeline provides measurable improvements in resolving syntactic ambiguity, achieving higher accuracy and relevance in complex query scenarios. The concept of a Utility Threshold was validated, indicating that quantum advantage becomes significant only beyond a certain level of linguistic complexity. For simpler queries, classical methods remain more efficient, reinforcing the importance of a hybrid architecture.

Furthermore, the study highlights critical insights into the limitations of traditional evaluation metrics, such as the Linearity Trap, where high lexical similarity does not necessarily correspond to semantic correctness. The QRAG framework addresses this issue through contextual realignment, ensuring that the generated responses are both relevant and factually accurate.

Overall, this work establishes that quantum computing, when applied selectively, can significantly enhance the capabilities of RAG systems. Rather than replacing classical approaches, the integration of quantum methods provides a complementary mechanism for handling complex linguistic structures.
