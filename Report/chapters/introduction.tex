\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Background}

Large Language Models (LLMs) have significantly advanced the field of Natural Language Processing (NLP), demonstrating strong capabilities in tasks such as text generation, summarization, and question answering. However, these models rely on parametric knowledge acquired during training, which limits their ability to access updated information and often leads to issues such as hallucination and factual inconsistency.

To address these limitations, Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) has emerged as a hybrid paradigm that combines LLMs with external knowledge sources. In this approach, relevant documents are retrieved from a vector database and used as contextual input for the generation process. This integration improves factual grounding and enhances the reliability of generated responses.

\section{Problem Statement}

Despite its advantages, the effectiveness of RAG systems is heavily dependent on the quality of the retrieval process. A critical limitation arises when the system encounters syntactically ambiguous queries. Traditional retrieval mechanisms primarily rely on keyword matching or vector similarity, which fail to capture deeper structural relationships within language. As a result, ambiguous queries often lead to incorrect document retrieval and misleading outputs.

This issue becomes more pronounced in cases involving prepositional phrase attachment, reduced relative clauses, and other complex grammatical constructs. Classical NLP models tend to exhibit local attachment bias, where decisions are made based on nearby tokens rather than the global sentence structure, leading to semantic misinterpretation.

\section{Motivation}

The inability of classical RAG systems to resolve syntactic ambiguity highlights the need for more expressive computational frameworks. Quantum computing offers a promising direction due to its ability to represent and process information in high-dimensional Hilbert spaces. By leveraging principles such as superposition and entanglement, quantum systems can model complex dependencies that are difficult to capture using classical approaches.

This research is motivated by the hypothesis that quantum-enhanced models can complement classical retrieval systems, particularly in scenarios involving non-linear linguistic structures. Rather than replacing existing systems, the goal is to design a hybrid architecture that utilizes the strengths of both paradigms.

\section{Proposed Approach}

In this work, we propose the Quantum Retrieval-Augmented Generation (QRAG) framework, which integrates classical RAG pipelines with Quantum Natural Language Processing (QNLP) techniques. The system introduces an Ambiguity Controller that identifies structurally complex queries and dynamically routes them to a quantum processing pipeline.

The quantum component employs Variational Quantum Circuits (VQC) to encode grammatical dependencies and perform syntactic disambiguation. In contrast, simpler queries are handled using efficient classical methods, ensuring minimal computational overhead. This selective routing mechanism enables the system to balance accuracy and efficiency.

\section{Contributions}

The key contributions of this work are as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Identification of syntactic ambiguity as a critical limitation in classical RAG systems.
    \item Design of a hybrid Quantum-Classical framework for improved semantic understanding.
    \item Integration of Variational Quantum Classifiers for syntactic disambiguation.
    \item Development of an Ambiguity Controller for efficient query routing.
    \item Empirical validation demonstrating improved accuracy on ambiguous query datasets.
\end{itemize}
